Al-Muddaththir — The Cloaked One
Surah 74 · Juz 29 · Makkan — among the earliest revelations · 56 verses

Verses

Revealed

Period

Al-Muddaththir addresses the Prophet ﷺ wrapped in his cloak and rouses him with the command that opened his public mission: arise and warn. Five rapid directives follow — magnify your Lord, purify your garments, shun the idols, give without seeking gain, and be patient for your Lord's sake — before the scene leaps to the terror of the Trumpet and a Day that is anything but easy for the disbelievers. The surah then turns to a single man Allah created alone, to whom He gave extensive wealth and sons present at his side and a life made smooth — yet who schemed against the revelation, deliberating step by step (he thought, then looked, then frowned and scowled, then turned away in arrogance) until he pronounced his verdict: this is nothing but magic passed down, nothing but the word of a mortal. His sentence is Saqar, and over it are nineteen keepers.
The number nineteen becomes a sign that sorts hearts — a trial for the disbelievers, a source of certainty for the People of the Scripture, and an increase of faith for the believers, while none knows the hosts of your Lord but He. Oaths by the moon, the retreating night, and the brightening dawn seal the warning. Then the surah's moral core: every soul is held in pledge for what it earned, except the Companions of the Right, who in the Gardens ask the people of the Fire what drove them into Saqar — and receive a four-fold confession: we did not pray, we did not feed the poor, we indulged in vain talk, and we denied the Day of Recompense. The surah closes on those who flee the Reminder like frightened donkeys fleeing a lion, and on an open door: this is a reminder, so whoever wills may take heed.
The first public command — after the pause in revelation
After the first revelation in the cave and a pause (fatrat al-wahy), the Prophet ﷺ saw the angel again, came home trembling and said “cover me, cover me” (zammilūnī) — and then “Yā ayyuhā l-muddaththir, qum fa-andhir” was revealed. Where Iqra (96) marked the beginning of prophethood, qum fa-andhir (v.2) marked the start of the public call to warn.
— Narrated by Jābir ibn ʿAbdillāh, Bukhari 4922 (Book of Tafsīr, Sūrat al-Muddaththir); also Muslim.
Why they entered Saqar — vv.43–46
When the people of the Garden ask the people of the Fire what drove them there, the answer is four failures: “We were not of those who prayed (v.43), nor did we feed the poor (v.44), and we used to indulge in vain talk with those who indulged (v.45), and we used to deny the Day of Recompense (v.46).” The list moves from the right of Allah (prayer) to the right of people (feeding the poor) to the tongue and finally the root — denial of the Day. Neglecting prayer is named first.

Core message
The Prophet ﷺ, wrapped in his cloak, is roused with the command that launched the public mission: qum fa-andhir (arise and warn) (v.2). Five directives follow in rapid succession — magnify your Lord (v.3), purify your garments (v.4), shun ar-rujz, the idols and all defilement (v.5), do not give seeking to gain more in return (v.6), and be patient for your Lord's sake (v.7). Then the scene leaps forward: when the Trumpet (an-nāqūr) is blown (v.8), that Day will be ʿasīr — a difficult Day (v.9), for the disbelievers anything but easy (v.10).

Memory hook — one call, five commands, one Day
The section opens with the sleeper roused — yā ayyuhā l-muddaththir (v.1) — and a single imperative, qum fa-andhir (v.2). Then five staccato commands, each landing on the emphatic -ir/-ur rhyme: kabbir (magnify), ṭahhir (purify), uhjur (shun), and fa-ṣbir (be patient). Then the Day arrives with the blast of the Trumpet (v.8). One call → five commands → one Day.
Purify your garments — v.4
Wa thiyābaka fa-ṭahhir (and your garments purify) was understood by the mufassirin on two levels: the literal cleanliness of one's clothes, and — as Mujāhid and others noted — a metaphor for purifying the self, one's conduct, and the heart. A warner sent to people must himself be clean, inwardly and outwardly.
Give without seeking gain — v.6
Wa lā tamnun tastakthir (and do not confer a favour seeking to gain more) is read by the mufassirin as a call to pure sincerity: do not give a gift expecting a larger return, and do not let your good deeds feel like a favour you are owed for. The warner gives freely, for Allah alone.

Note on movement
Al-Muddaththir's 56 verses fall into short, hard-hitting movements: the opening call and its five commands, the Trumpet and the hard Day, the long portrait of the scheming man and his sentence to Saqar, the sign of the nineteen and the oaths, the moral reckoning of the people of the Fire, and the closing image of the fleeing donkeys. Reciters usually let the pace tighten through the scheming passage (vv.18–25) and soften at the open-door ending.
A
Full surah — the complete arc
Verses 1–56 · in one sitting
Reciting the whole surah preserves its sweep: the call to warn → the Trumpet → the man who schemed against revelation → Saqar and its nineteen → every soul in pledge → the fleeing donkeys and the open door.
The ending — huwa ahlu t-taqwā wa ahlu l-maghfira — is a balanced close on fear and hope, a fitting note to carry into ruku'.
Reflect as you go: the surah keeps asking what a heart does with a warning — the scheming man rejects it, the people of Saqar ignored it, and the closing verse leaves the door open to whoever wills.
B
Two-part split — common for qiyam
Split at v.30
Rak'ah 1 — vv.1–30: the call to arise and warn, the Trumpet and the hard Day, and the man created alone whose scheming ends in Saqar, closing on “over it are nineteen.”
Rak'ah 2 — vv.31–56: the wisdom of the nineteen, the oaths, every soul in pledge, the confession of the people of the Fire, and the closing reminder.
C
Three-part split — extended nights
Breaks at v.10 and v.37
Rak'ah 1 — vv.1–10: the cloaked one roused, the five commands, and the blast of the Trumpet on a Day hard for the disbelievers.
Rak'ah 2 — vv.11–37: the man created alone, his step-by-step scheming, Saqar, and the sign of the nineteen sealed by the oaths.
Rak'ah 3 — vv.38–56: every soul in pledge, the four confessions of the people of the Fire, and the fleeing donkeys giving way to the open door.
Natural stopping points
v.7
End of the five opening commands — wa li-rabbika fa-ṣbir. A clean break before the scene jumps forward to the Trumpet.
v.10
End of the Trumpet passage — the hard Day, not easy for the disbelievers. A settled landing before the portrait of the scheming man.
v.30
ʿalayhā tisʿata ʿashar — “over it are nineteen.” The end of the Saqar passage and a striking pause before its wisdom is unfolded.
v.37
End of the oaths and the warning to mankind — to whoever wills to advance or hold back. A natural pivot into the reckoning that follows.
v.48
fa-mā tanfaʿuhum shafāʿatu sh-shāfiʿīn — the close of the people of the Fire's confession. A heavy, complete unit before the final turn.
v.56
The final verse — He is worthy of fear and worthy of forgiveness. A balanced rhetorical close that needs nothing after it.
