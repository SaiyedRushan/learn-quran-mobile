19:T693,Al-Mursalaat opens with five rapid oaths — by those <em>sent forth</em> one after another, storming, spreading wide, then distinguishing truth from falsehood and delivering a reminder, whether as justification or as warning. The whole volley of oaths bends toward a single verdict: <em>what you are promised is to occur</em> (v.7). The mufassirin read these emissaries as the winds, the angels, or both — a chain of God's dispatched forces — and from them the surah turns to the day the promise lands: the stars obliterated, the sky torn open, the mountains scattered, the messengers gathered for their appointed hour. This is <em>yawm al-fasl</em>, the Day of Decision, and the surah asks, almost in disbelief, for what Day was all of this postponed?

From there the chapter is stitched together by one recurring line — <em>waylun yawma'idhin lil-mukadhdhibin</em> (woe, that Day, to the deniers) — repeated ten times as its spine, each time after a fresh proof. It recalls the earlier peoples that were destroyed; it traces your own making from a despised drop lodged in a firm place for a known term; it shows the earth as a <em>container</em> gathering the living and the dead. Then the guilty are driven to a threefold shadow of smoke that gives no shade and shields nothing from the flame, throwing sparks like fortresses. On that Day they will not speak, nor be allowed to excuse themselves.
