Al-Mursalaat — The Emissaries
Surah 77 · Juz 29 · Makkan · 50 verses
سُورَةُ المُرۡسَلَاتِ
Verses
50
Revealed
33rd
Period
Makkan
Juz

Al-Mursalaat opens with five rapid oaths — by those sent forth one after another, storming, spreading wide, then distinguishing truth from falsehood and delivering a reminder, whether as justification or as warning. The whole volley of oaths bends toward a single verdict: what you are promised is to occur (v.7). The mufassirin read these emissaries as the winds, the angels, or both — a chain of God's dispatched forces — and from them the surah turns to the day the promise lands: the stars obliterated, the sky torn open, the mountains scattered, the messengers gathered for their appointed hour. This is yawm al-fasl, the Day of Decision, and the surah asks, almost in disbelief, for what Day was all of this postponed?
From there the chapter is stitched together by one recurring line — waylun yawma'idhin lil-mukadhdhibin (woe, that Day, to the deniers) — repeated ten times as its spine, each time after a fresh proof. It recalls the earlier peoples that were destroyed; it traces your own making from a despised drop lodged in a firm place for a known term; it shows the earth as a container gathering the living and the dead. Then the guilty are driven to a threefold shadow of smoke that gives no shade and shields nothing from the flame, throwing sparks like fortresses. On that Day they will not speak, nor be allowed to excuse themselves. Against them stands the calm of the God-conscious among shades, springs, and fruits — eat and drink in satisfaction for what you used to do — until the surah closes with the question that hangs over every denier: after this, in what statement will they believe?
Structure — the tenfold refrain
The line waylun yawma'idhin lil-mukadhdhibin (“Woe, that Day, to the deniers”) recurs ten times — at v.15, 19, 24, 28, 34, 37, 40, 45, 47, and 49 — acting as the surah's spine. Each repetition falls after a distinct proof (a cosmic sign, the fate of earlier peoples, human creation, the earth, the fire, the silence of Judgement), so the same verdict lands again and again on those who deny.
Context — revealed in a cave
Ibn Mas'ud (may Allah be pleased with him) reported: “While we were with the Prophet ﷺ in a cave, Surat wal-Mursalati 'urfan was revealed to him, and he was reciting it and I was learning it from his mouth.” The surah was received and taught fresh from the Prophet's recitation.
— Sahih al-Bukhari 4930 (also 1830, 3317) & Sahih Muslim 2234a.

Core message
The surah swears by five ranks of emissaries: al-mursalat sent forth in succession, al-'asifat that storm, al-nashirat that spread wide, al-fariqat that separate truth from falsehood, and al-mulqiyat that cast down a reminder — 'udhran aw nudhran (as justification or as warning) (vv.1-6). The mufassirin explain these as the winds, the angels, or a mix of both. The oaths all point to one certainty: innama tu'aduna la-waqi' — what you are promised is bound to occur (v.7). Then the scene of that Day: the stars obliterated, the sky split open, the mountains blown away, the messengers gathered at their appointed time (vv.8-11) — postponed for the yawm al-fasl, the Day of Decision (vv.12-14). The first Woe, that Day, to the deniers falls (v.15).

Memory hook — five oaths, one verdict
The five oaths land in two waves you can hear in the Arabic: three joined by wa- / fa- describing motion ('urfan… 'asfan… nashran), then two describing purpose (fariqat… mulqiyat). Sent → storming → spreading → distinguishing → delivering. Every one of them bends toward the answer in v.7: la-waqi' — it will happen. Hold the chain of five, then the single word that closes it.
Yawm al-fasl — the Day of Decision — vv.13-14
Yawm al-fasl is the Day on which Allah separates and decides between creation — truth from falsehood, the wronged from the wrongdoer, believer from denier. The rhetorical wa-ma adraka ma yawmu l-fasl (what can make you know what the Day of Decision is?) in v.14 signals a reality beyond full grasp. It is the same Day the earlier oaths were sworn to establish.
The messengers' appointed hour — v.11
Wa-idha l-rusulu uqqitat — “when the messengers are given their appointed time.” On the Day of Decision the messengers are gathered to testify for and against their peoples (cf. 4:41). The cosmic upheaval of vv.8-10 is not chaos but the clearing of the stage for that testimony.

A surah of proofs and a refrain
Al-Mursalaat is built for recitation that breathes with its structure: bursts of proof, each sealed by the same solemn line — waylun yawma'idhin lil-mukadhdhibin. Feel the refrain return ten times, and let each one land after the sign that precedes it. It pairs naturally in study and recitation with its neighbours in Juz 29 (An-Naba', An-Nazi'at), all sounding the theme of the Day that is surely coming.
A
Ride the tenfold refrain
Verses 1-50 · hearing the surah's spine
The clearest way into Al-Mursalaat is to notice its recurring line — waylun yawma'idhin lil-mukadhdhibin — at v.15, 19, 24, 28, 34, 37, 40, 45, 47, and 49. Each time it returns, pause and recall the proof just recited that earns it.
The proofs move outward then inward: the emissaries and the cosmic signs, the peoples of old, your own creation, the earth, then the fire and the two tables of judgement. Let the refrain be the drumbeat that carries you from one to the next.
The final verse (v.50) deliberately breaks the pattern — no refrain follows it, only the unanswered question. Let that silence land.
B
Memorising by blocks
Verses 1-50 · four natural units
The refrain conveniently marks memorisation blocks. A workable split follows the four sections: the oaths and the Day of Decision (1-15), the proofs from history and creation (16-28), the guilty and the threefold shadow (29-40), and the reward and closing question (41-50).
Within each block, learn the proof first, then attach the refrain — the repeated line is identical every time, so the effort goes into the verses between the repetitions.
Take care with the sequence of refrains: because the line never changes, the discipline is remembering which proof comes before each one, not the refrain itself.
Natural stopping points
v.15
End of the opening oaths and the scene of the Day of Decision, sealed by the first refrain. A clean break before the turn to earlier peoples.
v.28
End of the proofs from history, human creation, and the earth — three a-lam (did We not…?) arguments, each closed by the refrain. A strong mid-surah landing.
v.40
End of the scene of the guilty driven to the threefold shadow and struck silent, closing on the challenge 'then scheme against Me' and its refrain.
v.50
The final verse — the closing question that follows no refrain. A rhetorical seal to the whole surah; the natural end of the recitation.
