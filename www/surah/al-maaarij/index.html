Al-Ma'aarij — The Ascending Stairways
Surah 70 · Juz 29 · Makkan · 44 verses

Al-Ma'aarij opens with a taunt made into a prayer: a mocker demands that the promised punishment fall — right now, if it is real. The surah answers not by hurrying the punishment but by naming its Source: it is from Allah, Owner of the ascending ways (dhi l-ma'ārij), the One to whom the angels and the Spirit ascend on a Day whose measure is fifty thousand years. To the mockers that Day looks impossibly distant; to Allah it is near. So the Prophet ﷺ is told: be patient with a beautiful patience. Then the terror is unveiled — a sky like molten metal, mountains like tufts of wool, and a moment so overwhelming that no close friend even asks after his friend, and the guilty man would ransom himself from the punishment with his own children, his wife, his brother, his whole clan, everyone on earth — anything to escape. But no: it is Lazā, the Flame that strips away the very skin.
From that scene the surah turns to diagnose the human heart itself: man was created halū' — anxious and grasping — panicking when evil touches him and hoarding when good does. There is one exception, and the surah paints it in careful strokes: the musallīn — those constant in prayer, who set aside a known right in their wealth for the beggar and the deprived, who affirm the Day of Recompense and fear their Lord's punishment, who guard their chastity, keep their trusts and testimonies, and carefully maintain their prayer. Those will be in Gardens, honoured. The surah closes by turning back to the disbelievers rushing toward the Prophet in scattered groups — do they really expect Paradise while they deny? — and leaves them to their vain amusement until the Day they are dragged from their graves, eyes downcast, humiliation covering them: the very Day they were promised.
Portrait of the saved — the musallīn (vv.22–35)
Against the grasping, anxious nature of man, the surah carves out one exception and describes it in detail: illā l-musallīn (except the observers of prayer). The passage opens and closes with prayer (v.23 and v.34) and holds between them a full portrait of faith in action — a known right in wealth for the needy, belief in the Day of Recompense, fear of the Lord, chastity, faithfulness to trusts and testimonies. It is one of the Qur'an's clearest checklists of the believing character, and its reward is stated plainly: ulā'ika fī jannātin mukramūn — those will be in Gardens, honoured (v.35).
Context — the one who demanded the punishment
The mufassirūn relate that the opening verses came in response to one of the Makkan chiefs who mockingly demanded that the threatened punishment be sent down at once — echoing the challenge recorded in Sūrah al-Anfāl (8:32): “O Allah, if this be the truth from You, then rain down upon us stones from the sky.” Several reports name al-Naḍr ibn al-Ḥārith, and others Abū Jahl; the point of the sūrah stands regardless — the punishment they scorned is real, and its measure is with Allah alone.

Core message
A mocker demands the promised punishment — one that is wāqi' (bound to fall) upon the disbelievers, and which no one can push away (vv.1–2). The surah answers by naming its Source: it is from Allah, dhi l-ma'ārij (Owner of the ways of ascent), to whom the angels and the Spirit ascend on a Day whose measure is fifty thousand years (vv.3–4). So the Prophet ﷺ is commanded: fa-sbir sabran jamīlā — be patient with a beautiful patience (v.5). They see that Day as far off; Allah sees it as near (vv.6–7).

Memory hook — far to them, near to Him
The section pivots on a single contrast held in two short verses. In v.6 the disbelievers yarawnahu ba'īdā (see it as distant); in v.7 Allah narāhu qarībā (We see it as near). Distant / near — the same event, two utterly different measures of time. Anchor v.6 to v.7 and the surah's whole answer to the mocker is in your hand: the delay they read as safety is only the nearness they cannot yet see.
Beautiful patience — v.5
Sabran jamīlā (a beautiful patience) is patience without complaint to the creation — bearing the mockery and the wait while turning only to Allah. It is the same phrase Ya'qūb used over the loss of Yūsuf. Facing those who taunt the very promise of the Hereafter, the Prophet ﷺ is told not to argue the timing but to endure with grace, because the measure of the Day belongs to Allah alone.
The Day of fifty thousand years — v.4
On that Day ta'ruju l-malā'ikatu wa-l-rūh — the angels and the Spirit (Jibrīl) ascend to Him — and its miqdār (measure) is fifty thousand years. The mufassirūn understand this as the awesome length of the Day of Judgement as experienced by those held to account, while it is made light for the believer. The surah's very name, al-Ma'ārij (the ascending ways), is drawn from this scene of ascent.

A surah of warning and patience
Al-Ma'aarij is a Makkan surah that pairs a sober picture of the Last Day with a portrait of the believing character. Its natural home is in reflection: recite it when you need to steady patience against mockery or hardship (v.5), and let the portrait of the musallīn (vv.22–35) serve as a mirror for your own prayer, charity, chastity, and trust. It sits close to Al-Mulk and Al-Haqqah in the mushaf and shares their rhythm of question, scene, and answer.
A
Full surah in one sitting
Verses 1–44 · to keep its arc intact
At forty-four short verses, Al-Ma'aarij reads comfortably in one sitting and rewards being taken whole: the demand for punishment → the Day of ascent and beautiful patience → the terror where no friend asks after friend → man's anxious nature → the portrait of those who pray → the mockers left to their promised Day.
Recite v.5 — fa-sbir sabran jamīlā — slowly when the surah's warnings feel heavy; it is the calm centre the rest of the surah revolves around.
Let the portrait of the musallīn (vv.22–35) land as a checklist, not just a scene — prayer, a known right for the poor, belief in the Day, fear of Allah, chastity, trusts, testimony, prayer again.
B
For memorisation
Verses 1–44 · four natural blocks
The four sections give clean review blocks: 1–7 (the demand and the ascending ways), 8–18 (the Day and the Flame), 19–35 (man's nature and the saved), 36–44 (the mockers and the promised Day). Revise one block per sitting, then join them.
The rhyme shifts help memory: the early verses end on a long -ā (wāqi', dāfi', jamīlā), the middle passage on the light -an of jazū'ā / manū'ā, and the musallīn passage on the -ūn plural (dā'imūn, mushfiqūn, mukramūn).
Watch the two prayer-verses that frame vv.22–35 — dā'imūn (v.23) and yuhāfizūn (v.34) — as bookends; fixing them first makes the list between them easier to hold.
Natural stopping points
v.7
End of the opening answer to the mocker — they see the Day as far, Allah sees it as near. A clean break before the Day itself is unveiled.
v.18
Close of the Judgement scene — the Flame and whom it calls. The passage (vv.8–18) is a complete unit and ends on a heavy, settled note.
v.35
End of the portrait of the saved — ulā'ika fī jannātin mukramūn (those will be in Gardens, honoured). A strong, luminous landing before the turn back to the mockers.
v.44
The final verse — the promised Day, eyes downcast and humiliation covering them. A closing that needs no answer.
