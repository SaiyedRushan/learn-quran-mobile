Their own words fill the first half of the surah. They call it <em>an amazing Qur'an</em> that guides to what is right, and on the strength of it they declare their faith and vow never again to associate anyone with their Lord. They testify to the pure oneness of Allah — that <em>exalted is the nobleness of our Lord; He has not taken a wife or a son</em> — and they confess how their own foolish ones had spread lies about Him and how men who sought refuge in jinn had only made matters worse. They describe the heaven they once eavesdropped upon, now filled with stern guards and burning flames since the Prophet's mission began, and they admit that they are of divided ways: among them are the righteous, and among them are others — the ones who submit seek right guidance, while the unjust are firewood for Hell.

The second half turns from the jinn's testimony to direct revelation. Steadfastness on the straight path is promised abundant provision, while turning away from the remembrance of one's Lord leads to arduous punishment. The mosques belong to Allah alone, so none is to be invoked beside Him — and when the servant of Allah stood calling upon Him, the jinn nearly pressed upon him in crowds to listen.
