Al-Qiyaama — The Resurrection
Surah 75 · Juz 29 · Makkan · 40 verses
سُورَةُ القِيَامَةِ
Verses

Period

Al-Qiyaama opens with a double oath — by the Day of Resurrection and by an-nafs al-lawwāmah, the self-reproaching soul that blames itself for its sins — sworn to answer the one doubt the disbeliever cannot shake: that these scattered bones will ever be gathered again. Allah replies that He is not merely able to reassemble the skeleton, but to re-form even the fingertips, the fine ridges of banānahu, exactly as they were. Man's denial, the surah reveals, is not really a problem of evidence — man desires to continue in sin, so he asks, mockingly, when is the Day of Resurrection? The answer comes as a scene: when the sight is dazzled, the moon eclipsed, and the sun and moon are joined, man will cry ayna l-mafarr — where is the escape? — and hear the verdict, no refuge; to your Lord that Day is the return. There he will be informed of everything he sent ahead and left behind, a witness against his own soul even as he offers his excuses.
Into the middle of this the surah places a striking parenthesis addressed to the Prophet ﷺ himself: do not move your tongue to hasten the Qur'an — its gathering in your heart and its recitation are upon Us (vv.16–19). Then the diagnosis returns: you love al-'ājilah, the fleeting life you can grasp now, and leave the Hereafter. Two kinds of faces are set side by side — some radiant, looking toward their Lord, others gloomy, sensing a back-breaking calamity. The surah then draws close to death itself: the soul reaching the collarbone, the desperate cry who can cure him?, the legs entwined as the world is left behind and the procession to the Lord begins. It closes on the man who neither believed nor prayed but denied and turned away — and on the argument that silences him: the One who made him from a mere sperm-drop and fashioned him into pairs, male and female, is He not able to give life to the dead?
How revelation came — vv.16–19
In the middle of a scene about Judgment, Allah turns to comfort the Prophet ﷺ: lā tuharrik bihi lisānak — do not move your tongue to rush the Qur'an; its collection in your heart and its recitation are upon Us. Ibn 'Abbas explained that the Prophet ﷺ, out of fear of forgetting, would move his lips as Gabriel recited — so Allah promised to preserve it in his heart and let him recite it back afterwards.
— Sahih al-Bukhari 5 (Ibn 'Abbas), also in the tafsir of this surah in Bukhari & Muslim.
Looking at their Lord — vv.22–23
Wujūhun yawma'idhin nādirah, ilā rabbihā nāzirah — some faces, that Day, will be radiant, looking toward their Lord. Ahl al-Sunnah take this as clear proof of the believers' vision of Allah in the Hereafter, confirmed by the hadith: you will see your Lord as you see the full moon, without crowding to see it.
— Vision report: Sahih al-Bukhari 7434 & Sahih Muslim 633 (Jarir ibn 'Abdullah), ṣaḥīḥ.

Core message
The surah opens with two oaths: by the Day of Resurrection, and by an-nafs al-lawwāmah — the soul that reproaches itself (vv.1–2). Both point to the same reality: a day of reckoning, and a conscience within that already knows it. Then the doubt is named and answered: does man think We will never gather his bones? Yes We will — and We are able to re-form even the ridges of his fingertips (vv.3–4). The real root of his denial is not lack of proof but appetite: he wants to keep sinning freely with the Day out of view (v.5).

Memory hook — two oaths, then the fingertips
Anchor the opening on a contrast of scale. The oath is by something vast — yawm al-qiyāmah (the Day of Resurrection) — and something intimate — an-nafs al-lawwāmah (the self-blaming soul). Then the answer to the doubt lands on the smallest detail imaginable: not just 'izāmahu (his bones) reassembled, but banānahu (his fingertips) re-proportioned. Vast Day, blaming soul, and the fingerprint — big, near, and tiny, all held by the same power.
The self-reproaching soul — v.2
An-nafs al-lawwāmah is the conscience that turns on its owner — regretting evil done and good left undone. The scholars contrast it with an-nafs al-ammārah (the soul that commands evil) and an-nafs al-mutma'innah (the soul at peace, named in Al-Fajr 89:27). That it is worthy of an oath tells you this inner reproach is a mercy: the pang of guilt is itself a sign pointing toward the Day.
Why the fingertips? — v.4
The verse could have stopped at reassembling the bones; instead it names banānahu — the fingertips. Many later scholars noted the fittingness of the detail: the fine ridges of each fingertip are unique to every human being. The One who can restore even those individual whorls, indistinguishable to us, is beyond being defeated by a scattered skeleton.

A surah for softening the heart
Al-Qiyaama is short, vivid, and built to be felt. Its scenes — the scattered bones reassembled, the dazzled eye, the two faces, and above all the soul rising to the collarbone — are among the most affecting in the Qur'an on death and the return to Allah. Recite it slowly enough to picture each scene; it rewards pausing over the deathbed passage (vv.26–30) in particular.
A
The whole surah in one sitting
Verses 1–40 · a single, unbroken arc
At forty short verses, Al-Qiyaama recites comfortably in one rak'ah or one sitting, and reciting it whole preserves its shape: the oath and the reassembled bones → the Day and the witness → the parenthesis on revelation → the two faces → the deathbed → the closing argument from man's own origin.
Let the rhyme carry you: long stretches end in an open -ā sound (tawallā, yatamattā, sallā, sudā, sawwā), which makes the surah both easy to memorise and moving to hear.
Reflect as you go — this is a surah of scenes, not abstractions. Slow down at each image and let it land before moving to the next.
B
For memorisation — six natural blocks
Verses 1–5, 6–15, 16–19, 20–25, 26–30, 31–40
The six sections give clean review blocks. The shortest, vv.16–19 (the passage on how revelation was received), is a good place to begin because of its distinctive theme and repeated 'alaynā (upon Us).
The deathbed passage (vv.26–30) and the closing origin-argument (vv.37–40) are the emotional peaks — memorise them with their meanings so the recitation stays a reflection, not just sounds.
Watch the two near-identical warnings in vv.34–35 (awlā laka fa-awlā, thumma awlā laka fa-awlā) — the second simply prefixes thumma (then). Fix the order early.
Natural stopping points
v.5
End of the opening — the oaths, the answer over the bones and fingertips, and the naming of man's real motive. A clean break before the scene of the Day.
v.15
End of the Judgment scene — 'where is the escape?', no refuge but the Lord, and man as witness against himself. A settled pause before the parenthesis on revelation.
v.19
End of the self-contained passage on how the Qur'an was given (vv.16–19), closing on 'upon Us is its clarification.' A natural return to the main theme.
v.25
End of the two-faces contrast — radiant faces looking to their Lord, gloomy faces bracing for the blow. A strong landing before the turn to death.
v.30
End of the deathbed passage — the soul at the collarbone and the driving-onward to the Lord. A heavy, complete unit; a powerful place to pause.
v.40
The final verse — the closing question that needs no answer: is the One who made you from a drop not able to give life to the dead? The perfect rhetorical ending.
