19:T65e,Al-Insaan opens on the smallest and most humbling of questions: was there not <em>a period of time</em> when man was not even a thing worth mentioning? From that nothingness Allah brought him into being from a <em>mingled sperm-drop</em>, gave him hearing and sight, and — the whole purpose — guided him to the way, leaving him to walk it as one who is <em>grateful</em> or one who is <em>ungrateful</em>. The two roads then part sharply: for those who deny, chains, shackles, and a blaze; but for the righteous (<em>al-abrar</em>), a cup whose mixture is camphor, drawn from a spring that Allah's servants make gush forth as they please.

The surah lingers lovingly on who these righteous are, and what they did with the fleeting world. They fulfil their vows, fear a Day whose evil is far-reaching, and feed the needy, the orphan, and the captive — feeding them <em>for the countenance of Allah alone</em>, wanting from them neither reward nor thanks. Their recompense fills the second half: a garden and silk, reclining on couches in perfect shade, served from vessels of silver and cups of ginger from the spring named Salsabīl, waited on by ageless youths like scattered pearls, and given by their Lord a <em>purifying drink</em>. The surah closes by turning to the Messenger ﷺ: the Qur'an was sent down in stages, so <em>be patient</em>, remember your Lord morning and evening, and prostrate to Him through the long night — for these people love the fleeting world and leave a heavy Day behind them.
