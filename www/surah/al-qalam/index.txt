19:T772,Al-Qalam belongs to the earliest days of the mission, when the Makkans were calling the Prophet ﷺ a madman to turn people away from him. The surah answers that slander head-on. It opens with the disjointed letter <em>Nun</em> and a solemn oath — by the pen and what they inscribe — and then delivers its verdict directly: <em>you are not, by the favour of your Lord, a madman</em>. Far from it: for him is an unending reward, and he stands upon a <em>khuluqin 'azim</em>, a magnificent character. The disbelievers wish he would soften so they could soften, but he is told not to yield to the deniers — and a portrait is drawn of the vile schemer working against him: a habitual swearer, a scorner, a spreader of slander, cruel, and base, whose only credentials are wealth and sons and whose only answer to revelation is “legends of the ancients.” He will be branded on the snout.

The surah then tells the parable of the owners of the garden, who swore to harvest at dawn and shut the poor out of their due — and woke to find their garden reaped bare by a visitation in the night, a warning that arrogance and stinginess invite ruin. From there it turns to the Hereafter: the righteous have the Gardens of Pleasure, and Allah will never treat those who submit like the criminals. A cascade of challenges silences the disbelievers' claims, and then comes the terrifying scene of the Day the shin is uncovered, when they are called to prostrate and cannot. It closes with counsel to the Prophet ﷺ: be patient for your Lord's decree, and do not be like the companion of the fish — Yunus — who called out in distress; had a favour from his Lord not reached him, he would have been cast onto the barren shore, blamed. The final verses return to where it began: they nearly strike him down with their glaring eyes and call him mad — but it is nothing less than a reminder to all the worlds.1a:T45d,Allah tests the Makkans as He once tested the owners of a garden (v.17): they swore to harvest its fruit at daybreak, cutting the poor out of their share, and made no exception — no <em>insha'Allah</em> (v.18). While they slept, a visitation from their Lord swept over it (v.19), and by morning it was stripped bare as if already reaped (v.20). They called to one another at dawn (v.21) — “go early to your crop if you would harvest” (v.22) — setting off whispering (v.23) so that no poor person could enter upon them that day (v.24), confident in their power to withhold (v.25). But when they saw it, they cried, “we are lost — no, we are deprived!” (vv.26–27). The most balanced among them said, “did I not tell you to glorify Allah?” (v.28); they answered, “glory be to our Lord — we were wrongdoers” (v.29), and turned to blame one another (v.30). “Woe to us — we were transgressors” (v.31); “perhaps our Lord will give us better in exchange — to Him we turn in hope” (v.32).
