Al-Qalam — The Pen
Surah 68 · Juz 29 · Makkan — early Makkan period · 52 verses

Verses

Al-Qalam belongs to the earliest days of the mission, when the Makkans were calling the Prophet ﷺ a madman to turn people away from him. The surah answers that slander head-on. It opens with the disjointed letter Nun and a solemn oath — by the pen and what they inscribe — and then delivers its verdict directly: you are not, by the favour of your Lord, a madman. Far from it: for him is an unending reward, and he stands upon a khuluqin 'azim, a magnificent character. The disbelievers wish he would soften so they could soften, but he is told not to yield to the deniers — and a portrait is drawn of the vile schemer working against him: a habitual swearer, a scorner, a spreader of slander, cruel, and base, whose only credentials are wealth and sons and whose only answer to revelation is “legends of the ancients.” He will be branded on the snout.
The surah then tells the parable of the owners of the garden, who swore to harvest at dawn and shut the poor out of their due — and woke to find their garden reaped bare by a visitation in the night, a warning that arrogance and stinginess invite ruin. From there it turns to the Hereafter: the righteous have the Gardens of Pleasure, and Allah will never treat those who submit like the criminals. A cascade of challenges silences the disbelievers' claims, and then comes the terrifying scene of the Day the shin is uncovered, when they are called to prostrate and cannot. It closes with counsel to the Prophet ﷺ: be patient for your Lord's decree, and do not be like the companion of the fish — Yunus — who called out in distress; had a favour from his Lord not reached him, he would have been cast onto the barren shore, blamed. The final verses return to where it began: they nearly strike him down with their glaring eyes and call him mad — but it is nothing less than a reminder to all the worlds.
The magnificent character — v.4
“And indeed, you are of a great moral character” (wa innaka la-'ala khuluqin 'azim). When 'A'isha (may Allah be pleased with her) was asked about the character of the Prophet ﷺ, she answered: “His character was the Qur'an.” He lived what was revealed — this verse of praise is the standard by which the whole Sunnah of manners is measured.
— 'A'isha's report: Sahih Muslim 746, graded ṣaḥīḥ.
The evil eye is real — v.51
“Those who disbelieve would almost make you slip with their eyes when they hear the message” (v.51) is understood by many mufassirin to allude to the harm of the envious eye. The reality of the evil eye is firmly established: the Prophet ﷺ said, “The evil eye is real (al-'aynu haqq).” The remedy taught in the Sunnah is seeking refuge in Allah and reciting the protective adhkar, not superstition.
— Sahih al-Bukhari 5740 & Sahih Muslim 2187, ṣaḥīḥ.

Core message
The surah opens with the letter Nun and an oath by the pen and what is written with it (v.1) — then answers the Makkan slander directly: by the favour of his Lord, the Prophet ﷺ is not a madman (v.2); for him is a reward without end (v.3); and he stands upon a magnificent character (v.4). Soon both sides will see which of them is the truly deranged one (vv.5–6), for Allah alone knows who has strayed and who is guided (v.7). So do not yield to the deniers (v.8): they wish he would compromise so they could compromise in return (v.9). Then a scathing portrait of the schemer against him — an oath-swearing, scorning, slander-carrying, good-withholding, sinful, cruel, and base man (vv.10–13) — whose arrogance rests only on wealth and sons (v.14), who dismisses revelation as “legends of the ancients” (v.15). He will be branded on the snout (v.16).

Because he is a possessor of wealth and children, When Our verses are recited to him, he says, "Legends of the former peoples." We will brand him upon the snout.
Memory hook — three gifts, then the vices list
Hold the opening as three gifts to the Prophet ﷺ, one per verse: v.2 — you are not mad (ma anta bi-majnun); v.3 — an unending reward (ajran ghayra mamnun); v.4 — a great character (khuluqin 'azim). Then flip to the opposite: vv.10–13 stack the vices of his enemy like beads on a string — hallaf (swearer), hammaz (scorner), mashsha'in bi-namim (slanderer), manna'in lil-khayr (miser), 'utull (cruel), zanim (base). Gifts on one side, vices on the other.
His character was the Qur'an — v.4
Khuluqin 'azim (a magnificent character) is the highest praise given to any human in the Qur'an. 'A'isha (may Allah be pleased with her) summed it up when she said his character was the Qur'an — he embodied its every command and prohibition. In a surah defending him from the charge of madness, the counter-evidence Allah offers is not a miracle but his manners: the man they mocked was the most noble in conduct they had ever known.
Branded on the snout — v.16
Sanasimuhu 'ala l-khurtum — “We will brand him upon the snout.” The word khurtum is used for the trunk or snout of an animal; applying it to a proud human face is a deliberate humiliation, stripping the arrogant of the dignity he claimed. It is a mark of disgrace that will single him out, in this world or the next.

Note on flow
Al-Qalam moves through four clear movements: the defence of the Prophet ﷺ and the portrait of his enemy, the parable of the ruined garden, the Hereafter's two ends with the great challenges, and the closing counsel of patience. Reciters usually let the tone shift with each — firm in the defence, narrative in the parable, weighty at the shin, and gentle in the final counsel — and the natural stops fall on the seams between them.
A
Full surah — single sitting
Verses 1–52 · the complete arc
Reciting the whole surah preserves its frame: it opens and closes on the charge of “madness” (vv.2 and 51), answered finally by wa ma huwa illa dhikrun lil-'alamin — “it is but a reminder to the worlds.”
As the second surah revealed after Al-'Alaq, it pairs naturally with the earliest Makkan chapters in review — its subject is the very beginning of the mission and the opposition it met.
Let the parable of the garden (vv.17–33) breathe as a story with a beginning, middle, and end; it is the emotional centre of the surah.
B
Two-part split — common for qiyam
Split at v.33
Rak'ah 1 — vv.1–33: the defence of the Prophet ﷺ, the portrait of the slanderer, and the full parable of the garden, closing on kadhalika l-'adhab — “such is the punishment.”
Rak'ah 2 — vv.34–52: the reward of the righteous, the challenges, the Day the shin is uncovered, and the counsel of patience ending on the reminder to the worlds.
C
Four-part split — extended qiyam nights
Breaks at v.16, v.33, and v.45
Rak'ah 1 — vv.1–16: the pen, the Prophet's defence, and the branding of the arrogant slanderer.
Rak'ah 2 — vv.17–33: the parable of the owners of the garden, from their scheme to their regret.
Rak'ah 3 — vv.34–45: the two ends, the volley of challenges, and the uncovered shin.
Rak'ah 4 — vv.46–52: patience, the companion of the fish, and the reminder to the worlds.
Natural stopping points
v.7
End of the direct defence — your Lord knows best who has strayed and who is guided. A clean close before the command not to obey the deniers.
v.16
sanasimuhu 'ala l-khurtum — the branding of the slanderer. The portrait of the enemy is complete before the parable begins.
v.33
wa la-'adhabu l-akhirati akbar — the seal of the garden parable, linking its worldly ruin to the greater punishment to come.
v.45
wa umli lahum inna kaydi matin — the end of the challenge-and-warning passage, just before the final counsel to the Prophet ﷺ.
v.52
wa ma huwa illa dhikrun lil-'alamin — the final verse, overturning the charge of madness. A complete, resounding close.
